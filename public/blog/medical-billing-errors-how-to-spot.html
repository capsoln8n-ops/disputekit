8 Common Medical Billing Errors and How to Spot Them

Published 2026-03-12 · 7 min read · By BillFight AI Team

Medical billing is one of the most error-prone systems in the country. Studies from organizations like the Medical Billing Advocates of America suggest that up to 80% of medical bills contain at least one error. These errors almost always favor the provider — meaning you're being overcharged.

Here are the eight most common billing errors and how to catch them.

1. Duplicate Charges

The same service, test, or supply billed twice. This is the most common billing error and is especially prevalent with lab work, medications administered during a hospital stay, and daily room charges. How to spot it: compare each line item on your itemized bill. Look for identical CPT codes, dates, and amounts.

2. Upcoding

Being charged for a more expensive procedure or service than what was actually performed. For example, being billed for a Level 5 emergency room visit (the most complex and expensive) when your visit was a Level 3. How to spot it: compare the procedure codes on your bill to your medical records. If the description doesn't match what happened, it may be upcoded.

3. Unbundling

When a group of procedures that should be billed under a single comprehensive code are instead billed as separate individual charges (which cost more). For example, a blood panel that should be one charge is split into 10 separate test charges. How to spot it: look for clusters of related charges that seem like they should be one service.

4. Incorrect Patient Information

Wrong insurance ID, policy number, date of birth, or name can cause claims to be denied — leaving you with the full bill. How to spot it: check every piece of identifying information on your bill and EOB. Even a single digit error can cause a denial.

5. Services Not Rendered

Charges for tests, procedures, medications, or supplies that were ordered but never actually delivered or administered. This happens more often than you'd think, especially during hospital stays. How to spot it: cross-reference your bill against your medical records and your own recollection of what happened.

Stat: The National Health Care Anti-Fraud Association estimates that healthcare fraud (including billing errors and intentional fraud) costs the U.S. $68 billion annually.

6. Wrong Diagnosis Codes

An incorrect ICD-10 diagnosis code can make a covered service look non-covered, resulting in a denial. For example, if your doctor treated you for chest pain but the coder entered a code for a routine checkup, insurance might deny the claim. How to spot it: ask your provider for the diagnosis codes used and verify they match your actual condition.

7. Balance Billing Errors

Being billed for the difference between the provider's charge and insurance's allowed amount when you shouldn't be. If your provider is in-network, they've agreed to accept the insurance company's allowed amount as full payment. How to spot it: compare your EOB's "patient responsibility" amount to what the provider is billing you.

8. Operating Room Time Errors

Hospitals charge by the minute for operating room use. If your surgery took 45 minutes but you're billed for 90 minutes, that could be thousands of dollars in overcharges. How to spot it: request the anesthesia records, which document exact start and end times, and compare to the OR charges.

What to Do When You Find an Error

  1. Request a fully itemized bill (not a summary)
  2. Get copies of your medical records for the date(s) of service
  3. Document each error with specific line items and correct information
  4. Submit a written dispute to the billing department
  5. Follow up in writing every 14 days

Key Takeaway

Never pay a medical bill without reviewing it for errors first. The larger the bill, the more likely it contains mistakes. An itemized bill and your medical records are the two tools you need to catch most billing errors.
