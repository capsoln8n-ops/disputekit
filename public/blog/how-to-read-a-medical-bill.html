How to Read a Medical Bill: A Complete Breakdown

Published 2026-03-09 · 7 min read · By BillFight AI Team

Medical bills are notoriously confusing. They're filled with codes, abbreviations, and charges that seem designed to be incomprehensible. But understanding your bill is the first step to spotting errors and fighting overcharges.

Summary Statement vs. Itemized Bill

Most hospitals initially send a summary statement that shows a total amount due with very little detail. This is not enough information to verify accuracy. What you need is an itemized bill — a line-by-line breakdown of every charge. You have the legal right to request one.

Key Sections of a Medical Bill

Patient Information

Your name, date of birth, account number, and insurance information. Verify every detail — errors here can cause claim denials. Even a wrong digit in your insurance ID can result in you being billed the full amount.

Service Dates

The date(s) you received care. Check that these match your actual visits. Charges for dates you weren't at the facility are a red flag.

CPT Codes (Procedure Codes)

Current Procedural Terminology codes describe what was done. Every test, procedure, consultation, and service has a unique CPT code. For example, 99213 is a standard office visit, 99283 is a mid-level ER visit, and 36415 is a routine blood draw. These codes determine what you're charged.

ICD-10 Codes (Diagnosis Codes)

International Classification of Diseases codes describe why the service was needed. The diagnosis code must support the procedure code — if they don't match, insurance may deny the claim.

Charges

The provider's "list price" for each service. These are often dramatically higher than what insurance actually pays. Don't panic at the total charges — focus on what your insurance has applied and what your actual patient responsibility is.

Insurance Adjustments

The difference between what the provider charges and what your insurance has agreed to pay. If your provider is in-network, they've agreed to accept the insurance company's "allowed amount," which is typically 40-60% less than list price.

Insurance Payment

What your insurance company actually paid toward the bill. This comes after adjustments and is the amount the insurer sends directly to the provider.

Patient Responsibility

What you owe after insurance adjustments and payments. This is your actual bill. It includes your deductible (if not yet met), copay (fixed amount per visit), and coinsurance (your percentage of the allowed amount).

Key Takeaway

The number that matters is "Patient Responsibility" — not the total charges. Always wait for your insurance to process the claim before paying anything. If the patient responsibility amount seems wrong, compare it to your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) from your insurer.

Understanding Your EOB

Your Explanation of Benefits is not a bill — it's a statement from your insurance company showing how they processed your claim. It shows the billed amount, the allowed amount, what insurance paid, and what you owe. Always compare your EOB to the provider's bill. If the "patient responsibility" numbers don't match, something is wrong.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • Charges for dates you weren't treated
  • Services you don't recognize or remember receiving
  • The same charge appearing twice
  • An ER visit level higher than what you experienced
  • A "patient responsibility" amount higher than what your EOB shows
