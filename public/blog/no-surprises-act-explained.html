The No Surprises Act Explained: What It Covers and How to Use It

Published 2026-03-13 · 8 min read · By BillFight AI Team

The No Surprises Act is the most significant federal healthcare billing law in a generation. Signed into law in December 2020 and effective January 1, 2022, it fundamentally changes how surprise medical bills are handled in the United States.

But the law is complex, and many patients don't know how to actually use it. This guide breaks it down in plain English.

Who Does the No Surprises Act Protect?

The law protects anyone with private health insurance — including employer-sponsored plans, ACA marketplace plans, and individual plans. It does not apply to Medicare, Medicaid, TRICARE, or VA benefits (these programs have their own protections).

Uninsured and self-pay patients also get new protections: the right to a good faith cost estimate before receiving care.

What Situations Does It Cover?

Emergency Services

All emergency services are covered, regardless of whether the facility or providers are in-network. This includes the ER visit itself, any services provided during the emergency, and post-stabilization care until you can be safely transferred.

Out-of-Network Providers at In-Network Facilities

If you go to an in-network hospital but are treated by an out-of-network doctor (anesthesiologist, radiologist, pathologist, etc.), you're protected. You cannot be charged more than the in-network cost-sharing amount.

Air Ambulance Services

Out-of-network air ambulance providers cannot balance bill you. Your cost is limited to what you'd pay for in-network air ambulance services.

What the Law Actually Does

The No Surprises Act works in three key ways:

  • Caps your out-of-pocket cost — You pay only the in-network cost-sharing amount (copay, coinsurance, deductible) for covered surprise bills
  • Bans balance billing — Providers cannot send you a bill for the difference between their charge and what insurance pays
  • Creates a dispute process — Providers and insurers resolve payment disagreements through an Independent Dispute Resolution (IDR) process that doesn't involve you

Good Faith Estimates for Uninsured Patients

If you're uninsured or paying out of pocket, healthcare providers must give you a written "good faith estimate" of expected charges before your visit. If the final bill exceeds the estimate by $400 or more, you can dispute it through a patient-provider dispute resolution process.

Important: You must request the good faith estimate at least 3 business days before your scheduled service. For emergency care, the estimate isn't required (but the balance billing protections still apply).

How to Use the No Surprises Act

  1. Check your bill. Was it for emergency services, or from an out-of-network provider at an in-network facility? If yes, the law likely applies.
  2. Contact your insurer. Ask them to reprocess the claim under the No Surprises Act if it was billed at out-of-network rates.
  3. Write a dispute letter. If the provider is still billing you for the balance, send a formal letter citing the No Surprises Act (Public Law 117-169).
  4. File a complaint. Report violations to CMS at 1-800-985-3059 or online at cms.gov/nosurprises.

Key Takeaway

The No Surprises Act is powerful, but it only works if you invoke it. Providers won't automatically apply it — you need to know your rights and assert them. If you've received a surprise bill, check whether this law applies before paying anything.
