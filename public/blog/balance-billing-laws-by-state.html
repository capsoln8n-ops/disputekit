Balance Billing Laws by State: Know Your Protections

Published 2026-03-08 · 10 min read · By BillFight AI Team

While the federal No Surprises Act provides baseline protection against surprise medical bills, many states have their own balance billing laws that may offer even stronger protections. Some states had protections in place years before the federal law.

What Is Balance Billing?

Balance billing occurs when an out-of-network provider bills you for the difference between their charge and what your insurance pays. For example, if a doctor charges $5,000 for a procedure but your insurance only pays $2,000, the doctor might send you a "balance bill" for the remaining $3,000.

Federal vs. State Protections

The No Surprises Act covers emergency services, out-of-network providers at in-network facilities, and air ambulance services. State laws may go further — some states protect against balance billing in non-emergency situations, cover ground ambulances, or have more favorable dispute resolution processes.

Important: When both federal and state protections apply, the stronger protection wins. If your state's law is more protective than the No Surprises Act, the state law takes precedence.

States with Strong Protections

California

California's AB 72 (2017) prohibits balance billing for services at in-network facilities. The state's independent dispute resolution process has been in place since 2017. Patients pay only in-network cost-sharing amounts.

New York

New York's Emergency Medical Services and Surprise Bill Law (2015) was one of the first comprehensive surprise billing laws. It covers emergency services, surprise bills from out-of-network providers, and includes a robust independent dispute resolution process.

Texas

Texas SB 1264 (2019) provides strong protections for patients at in-network facilities who receive out-of-network care. It covers all state-regulated insurance plans and has a mediation process for billing disputes.

Florida

Florida's HB 221 (2016) protects patients from balance billing by out-of-network providers at in-network facilities for emergency and non-emergency services. It requires providers to accept the insurer's payment or enter a dispute resolution process.

Colorado

Colorado's HB 1174 (2020) provides comprehensive surprise billing protections and established an out-of-network billing dispute resolution process. It covers emergency and non-emergency situations.

States with Limited Protections

Some states only protect against balance billing in specific situations, such as emergency care only, or only for certain types of insurance plans. States with more limited protections include Alabama, Alaska, Wyoming, and several others.

How to Use Your State's Protections

  1. Identify your state's specific balance billing law
  2. Determine whether your insurance plan is state-regulated (most employer-sponsored plans are federally regulated under ERISA)
  3. Reference the specific state statute in your dispute letter
  4. File complaints with your state's Department of Insurance if the provider doesn't comply

Key Takeaway

Your state may offer stronger protections than the federal No Surprises Act. Always check both federal and state laws when disputing a balance bill. When writing your appeal letter, cite both sets of protections for maximum leverage.

ERISA Plans: A Key Exception

If your health insurance is through a large employer's self-funded plan, it may be regulated by federal ERISA law rather than state law. In this case, state balance billing laws don't apply — but the federal No Surprises Act still does. Check your plan documents or call your insurer to find out if your plan is self-funded.
