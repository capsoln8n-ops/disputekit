Medical Bill Statute of Limitations by State

Published 2026-03-01 · 7 min read · By BillFight AI Team

Every state has a time limit on how long a creditor can sue you to collect a debt, including medical bills. This is called the statute of limitations, and once it expires, the debt is legally unenforceable in court.

How Long Is the Statute of Limitations?

It varies by state, ranging from 3 to 10 years. Most states fall in the 3-6 year range. The clock typically starts from the date of your last payment or the date the debt became delinquent.

Important Warnings

Making a payment — even a small one — can restart the statute of limitations in many states. Before making any payment on old medical debt, check your state's rules. Also, even after the statute expires, the debt doesn't disappear. Collectors can still call you (subject to FDCPA rules), and the debt can still appear on your credit report for up to 7 years from the date of first delinquency.

Key Takeaway

Know your state's statute of limitations before engaging with collectors about old medical debt. If the debt is near or past the statute, think carefully before making any payment that could restart the clock.
