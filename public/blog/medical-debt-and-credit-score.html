How Medical Debt Affects Your Credit Score (2026 Rules)

Published 2026-03-06 · 7 min read · By BillFight AI Team

Medical debt and credit scores have a complicated relationship, and the rules have changed dramatically in recent years. If you're worried about a medical bill hurting your credit, there's good news: most medical debt no longer appears on credit reports under rules that took effect in 2023-2024.

The New Rules on Medical Debt and Credit

In a series of major changes, the three credit bureaus (Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion) eliminated medical collections under $500 from credit reports, extended the time before medical debt can appear on credit reports from 6 months to 12 months, and removed paid medical collection debt from credit reports entirely.

Additionally, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) has been pushing to eliminate all medical debt from credit reports.

What This Means for You

If your medical debt is under $500, it won't appear on your credit report at all. If it's over $500, you have a 12-month window to resolve it before it can appear. And if you pay it off (even through a payment plan), it will be removed.

When Medical Debt Still Hurts

Large medical debts ($500+) that remain unpaid for more than 12 months can still appear on your credit report and impact your score. Medical debt sent to a third-party collection agency can also affect your creditworthiness when applying for loans, even if the debt itself doesn't appear on your formal credit report.

Key Takeaway

Don't let fear of credit damage force you into paying a bill you believe is wrong. You have 12 months before medical debt can appear on your report, and disputing the bill during that time buys you even more protection. Use that window to fight unfair charges.

Steps to Protect Your Credit

  • Dispute any incorrect medical bills before the 12-month window closes
  • Negotiate a payment plan to prevent the account from going to collections
  • Apply for financial assistance or charity care
  • Check your credit reports regularly at annualcreditreport.com
  • Dispute any medical debt that appears on your credit report in error
