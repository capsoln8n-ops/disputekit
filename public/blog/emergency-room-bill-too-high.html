ER Bill Too High? How to Fight Emergency Room Charges

Published 2026-03-03 · 8 min read · By BillFight AI Team

Emergency room visits are the most expensive routine medical encounter in the U.S., with average costs ranging from $1,200 to $20,000+ depending on the severity. Worse, ER bills are among the most likely to contain errors and overcharges.

Why ER Bills Are So High

Emergency rooms charge a "facility fee" just for walking in the door, which can be $500-$3,000 before any treatment. On top of that, you'll receive separate bills from the ER physicians, lab work, imaging, and any specialists consulted. The ER also "triage levels" your visit on a 1-5 scale, with Level 5 being the most expensive. Upcoding (being assigned a higher level than warranted) is extremely common.

Check Your ER Level

The biggest variable in your ER bill is the "evaluation and management" (E/M) level. These range from CPT 99281 (Level 1, minor problem) to CPT 99285 (Level 5, life-threatening). A Level 5 visit costs 3-5x more than a Level 1. If you went to the ER for something relatively minor and were charged at Level 4 or 5, that's worth disputing.

Federal Protections for ER Visits

Under the No Surprises Act, all emergency services are protected regardless of network status. You cannot be balance billed for ER care, and your cost-sharing must be based on in-network rates. This applies even if the ER or the doctors treating you are out of network.

Steps to Fight Your ER Bill

  1. Request an itemized bill with all CPT and ICD-10 codes
  2. Verify the ER level matches your actual condition and treatment
  3. Compare charges to fair market rates
  4. Check for duplicate charges (very common in ER settings)
  5. Submit a written dispute for any errors or overcharges
  6. Apply for financial assistance if the bill is large

Key Takeaway

The ER is the most common source of both billing errors and surprise bills. Always request an itemized bill, check the E/M level, and don't pay without reviewing every charge.
