Insurance Claim Denied? How to Write a Winning Appeal

Published 2026-03-11 · 9 min read · By BillFight AI Team

Getting a health insurance claim denial can feel like a gut punch, especially when you're already dealing with a health issue. But here's what the insurance companies don't want you to know: denials are often reversed on appeal. In fact, studies show that 40-60% of appeals are successful.

Why Claims Get Denied

Understanding why your claim was denied is the first step to overturning it. The most common reasons include:

  • Prior authorization not obtained — your doctor didn't get pre-approval for the service
  • Service deemed "not medically necessary" — the insurer's medical reviewers disagree with your doctor
  • Out-of-network provider — the provider isn't in your plan's network
  • Coding errors — incorrect CPT or ICD-10 codes were submitted
  • Filing deadline missed — the claim wasn't submitted within the required timeframe
  • Benefit exclusion — the service isn't covered under your specific plan

The Three Levels of Appeal

Level 1: Internal Appeal

You have the right to an internal appeal with your insurance company. You must file within 180 days of receiving the denial (some plans allow less). Your appeal is reviewed by a different person than whoever made the original denial decision.

Level 2: External Review

If your internal appeal is denied, you can request an external review by an independent third party. This reviewer has no affiliation with your insurance company. Their decision is binding on the insurer.

Level 3: Regulatory Complaint / Legal Action

If both appeals fail, you can file complaints with your state's Department of Insurance, CMS (for marketplace plans), or pursue legal options.

Key Stat: According to healthcare data, patients who appeal insurance denials win 40-60% of the time on internal appeal and up to 70% on external review. Most people never appeal — don't be one of them.

How to Write Your Appeal Letter

Your appeal letter is the centerpiece of your case. It should be clear, factual, and well-documented. Include your policy information, the claim number, date of service, specific denial reason (from the EOB or denial letter), and why the denial is wrong.

For "Not Medically Necessary" Denials

Get a letter from your treating physician explaining why the service was necessary. Include relevant medical literature, clinical guidelines, and your medical history that supports the need for treatment.

For Coding Errors

Work with your provider's billing department to identify the correct codes and resubmit the claim. This is often the easiest type of denial to overturn.

For Prior Authorization Issues

If the service was an emergency or medically urgent, prior authorization may not have been required. Document the emergency nature of the care. If it wasn't an emergency, work with your doctor's office to obtain retroactive authorization.

Strengthen Your Appeal

  • Request your complete claim file from the insurer
  • Get a letter of medical necessity from your doctor
  • Include peer-reviewed studies supporting your treatment
  • Cite your plan's coverage terms (from the Summary of Benefits)
  • Note any state laws requiring coverage of your condition or treatment

Key Takeaway

An insurance denial is a decision, not a final answer. You have the legal right to appeal, and the statistics are in your favor. The key is to respond in writing with specific evidence addressing the exact reason for the denial.
