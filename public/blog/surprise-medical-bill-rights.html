Surprise Medical Bill? Know Your Rights and How to Fight Back

Published 2026-03-14 · 9 min read · By BillFight AI Team

You went to an in-network hospital, used your insurance, did everything right — and still got a bill for thousands of dollars from a doctor you never chose. Sound familiar? You're not alone. Surprise medical bills affect 1 in 5 emergency room visits and are one of the most common reasons Americans contact billing advocates.

The good news: federal law is now on your side. Here's what you need to know.

What Exactly Is a Surprise Medical Bill?

A surprise medical bill (also called a "balance bill") happens when you receive care from an out-of-network provider without knowing it. The most common scenarios include emergency room visits where attending physicians are out-of-network, anesthesiologists or radiologists at in-network hospitals who happen to be out-of-network, lab work sent to an out-of-network lab, and assistant surgeons or consultants called in during a procedure.

The Numbers: The average surprise medical bill in the U.S. is $2,424. Emergency room surprise bills average $822, while surgical surprise bills can reach $20,000+.

The No Surprises Act: Your Federal Shield

Effective January 1, 2022, the No Surprises Act provides sweeping protections against surprise medical bills. Under this law, you cannot be balance billed for emergency services regardless of network status, out-of-network providers at in-network facilities cannot charge you more than in-network cost-sharing, and air ambulance providers cannot balance bill you for out-of-network charges.

What This Means in Practice

If you receive a surprise bill covered by the No Surprises Act, your out-of-pocket cost should be no more than what you'd pay for an in-network provider. The provider and your insurance company must work out the payment between themselves through an Independent Dispute Resolution (IDR) process — you should not be in the middle.

Steps to Fight a Surprise Medical Bill

1. Verify It's Actually a Surprise Bill

Check your Explanation of Benefits (EOB) from your insurer. Was the provider out-of-network? Did you have a choice in selecting them? If an out-of-network provider treated you at an in-network facility, or you received emergency care, the No Surprises Act likely applies.

2. Contact Your Insurance Company

Call the number on the back of your insurance card and ask them to reprocess the claim under the No Surprises Act. Provide the date of service, facility name, and the out-of-network provider's information. Many insurers have dedicated teams for No Surprises Act claims.

3. File a Written Dispute

Send a formal dispute letter to both the provider and your insurer. Reference the No Surprises Act (Public Law 117-169), your state's balance billing protections, and the specific charges you're disputing.

4. File a Complaint if Needed

If the provider refuses to comply, file a complaint with the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) at 1-800-985-3059, your state's Department of Insurance, and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB).

State-Level Protections

Many states had surprise billing protections before the federal law. States like California, New York, Texas, Florida, and Colorado have their own laws that may offer additional protections beyond the No Surprises Act. Check your state's insurance department website for state-specific rules.

Key Takeaway

You should never pay a surprise medical bill without first checking whether the No Surprises Act or your state's balance billing laws apply. In most cases, your liability is limited to in-network cost-sharing amounts.

What If My Bill Predates the No Surprises Act?

Even for older bills not covered by the federal law, you still have options. Many states have their own protections, and you can always negotiate directly with the provider. Hospitals and providers regularly accept reduced payments, especially when you can demonstrate that the charges exceed fair market rates.
