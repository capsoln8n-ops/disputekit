Surprise Billing Protections by State: Your Complete Guide

Published 2026-02-26 · 9 min read · By BillFight AI Team

The federal No Surprises Act provides a baseline of protection, but many states have enacted their own surprise billing laws that may offer stronger protections. Understanding your state's specific rules can make the difference in a billing dispute.

States with Comprehensive Protections

Several states enacted comprehensive surprise billing laws before the federal government. These include California (AB 72, 2017), New York (Emergency Medical Services and Surprise Bill Law, 2015), Texas (SB 1264, 2019), Colorado (HB 1174, 2020), Florida (HB 221, 2016), Illinois (Network Adequacy and Transparency Act), Oregon (HB 2339), and Washington (Balance Billing Protection Act).

How State Laws May Be Stronger

Some state protections go beyond the federal law by covering ground ambulance services (the No Surprises Act only covers air ambulances), applying to state-regulated insurance plans that may not be covered federally, offering more favorable dispute resolution processes, or including broader definitions of what constitutes a "surprise bill."

Which Law Applies to You?

If your insurance plan is regulated by your state (most individual and small group plans), both federal and state laws apply and the stronger protection wins. If your plan is self-funded by your employer (regulated under federal ERISA), generally only the federal No Surprises Act applies.

Key Takeaway

Check both federal and state protections when fighting a surprise bill. Your state may offer broader coverage, different dispute resolution processes, or additional enforcement mechanisms.
